  • Best Italian Classic: Ferrari F8 Tributo (link to wiki)

    • This $270,530 coupe pays fitting tribute to the almost-sacred V8 engines that have powered the automaker’s most successful cars since its first mid-engine V-8, all the way back to the 1975 Ferrari 308 GTB. With a 3.9-liter, 711-horsepower engine, a perfect seven-speed gearbox, and 568 pound-feet of torque, it’s more powerful and efficient than its predecessor, the 488 GTB. The F8 Tributo weighs less than 3,000 pounds and is lethally quick: zero to 62 mph in 2.9 seconds, with style to spare.

  • Best Open-Top Thriller: Lamborghini Huracan Evo Spyder (link to wiki)

    • This is the mid-cycle refresh of the Huracan Spyder line, but it feels like the one you'd want out of the entire lot, thanks to its plus accoutrements for longer drives: ambient light packages, smartphone interfaces, electric seats, and “Lamborghini telemetry” for an enhanced driving feel as you round those hairpin turns. The trunk is small as a pillbox, and the chassis is stiff enough to make you sore; but the thrill of driving it removes any lingering doubt. The steering is so light and precise, it feels as if the car could tango across a tightrope; the brakes grab with the security of a bank vault. The $287,400 Huracan Evo does zero to 60 mph in 2.4 seconds. Top speed is 202 mph. Total power is 631hp, 29 hp more powerful than its siblings. Most important, it uses computer “logic” to sharpen or soften the response of the steering, the brakes, and the chassis, according to the style of the driver. The system makes 50 adjustments per second, based on 240 different inputs such as how the car rolls, pitches, accelerates, steers, and brakes; it anticipates desires, based on habits, and adjusts accordingly to changing conditions—much like a well-trained butler, but better looking.

  • Best Electric Sedan: Porsche Taycan Turbo S (link to wiki)

    • Porsche has hyped this car for four years, so there wasn't much room for error. It represents the lead portion of a $6 billion investment in electric for the company and is the most money the brand has spent on a single model's development, ever. The good news is that the $185,000 Taycan Turbo S delivers. It's fast, powerful, practical, and well-built. From behind the wheel and from the sidewalk, it feels and looks like a real car, not an appliance. The performance stats back it all up: 750 horsepower; zero to 60 mph in 2.6 seconds; two “gears” (one for strong power under lower speeds, the other for sustained efficiency at high speeds); and a range of 256 electric miles on a single battery charge. The only trouble here will be how long it takes to actually get one. While Porsche labors to keep up with demand in Zuffenhausen, Germany, waiting lists are already months long.

  • Best New Act: McLaren GT (link to wiki)

    • McLaren is jumping into a new segment here, a diversion from its usual track-focused, bare-minimum sports cars. The $210,000, 612-bhp GT is spacious, smoother on the road, and drop-dead sexy. It offers myriad new leather and cashmere trimmings in an interior made prominent with a spacious luggage bay and total-roof glass ceiling. The entertainment system has been improved, as has the stiffness of the chassis. Drive it for a day or a week; you'll find no aching backs here! Think of it as a lighter, less opulent grand tourer to the exceptional Bentley Continental GT.

  • Best Town Car: Bentley Flying Spur (link to wiki)

    • In an age of lighter, faster, quicker, sometimes it's nice to slow down, lay back into the coffers of wealth, and enjoy the ride. This $214,600 Flying Spur allows for that and more. Bentley reevaluated its iconic marque from the ground up this year, with a new grille, hood ornament, and crystal-cut LED headlights, 22-inch rims and diamond knurling throughout. The infotainment has been updated, and deep-plunging lambswool carpets and burled walnut panels throughout remain exceptional, as ever. It retains the same 6.0-liter, twin-turbocharged W12 engine as the Continental GT, with a dual-clutch, eight-speed transmission and the biggest iron brakes in the world. That's enough to do a zero to 60 mph sprint in 3.7 seconds, with a top speed of 207 mph.
